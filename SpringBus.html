SpringBus

Project Details

The Chicago public transportation system has a convenience problem. In this day and age public transportation has taken on a new form through Uber and Lyft, which allow users to order a ride for under $10 and have it there to pick them up minutes later.

To compete in a convenience centered world, the way that users find information about the bus schedule could use an update.


Problem:

The average person does not want to spend time deciphering the dust-covered schedule posted at the bus stop or navigating to a website when they are trying to find out how to get to their destination.

Solution:

I designed an app that makes it easy for users to:

  • Track their bus
  • Locate bus stops
  • Map out their route
  • Find the quickest route to their destination

Audience:

This app design was comissioned by the Chicago Public Transportation Authority and designed with the residents of the city and tourists in mind.

Learnings:

This project taught me a lot about color theory, specifically how to integrate several different colors that are not in the color scheme into a design without overwhelming the eye. I also learned that less is more when it comes to the use of color. The change in my use of color can most clearly be seen when you look at how my bus route bottons and drop down menus looked before I redisigned them with minimal use of color in mind. Seriously, looking back at my first designs for elements of this app is like looking back at pictures of me before I discovered leave-in conditioner.

Discover

This app was meant to be designed for the public transportation system of a big city, and while I have ridden public transportaion in large cities before as a tourist, I do not live in a large city. I rode the bus occasionally during college but the city I went to college in is best described using the term "urban sprawl". The goal of my research therefore, was to understand the pain points of residents of large cities who use the public bus system as their primary means of transportation.

Persona

I am designing this app for people like, Shameika; people who do not have a car and rely on the bus to get to work and to run errands. That is the group of Chicago's residents that would most often use the app and would benefit the most from it (with tourists being the other major group that will be helped by this app).

Competitive Analysis

I started my research by looking at two other public transportation apps, Transit and myStop.

My research for this case study involved gathering quantitative data from survey results and qualitative data both from open ended survey questions and from interviews. I also went on Reddit to read through the comments under posts made to discuss problems with public transportation. One of those posts from r/AskAnAmerican was entitled, If the public transport was good in your area, would you use it?

Survey Findings

According to my survey results, only 15% of the respondents used public buses as their primary form of transportation and when asked if they would still take the bus if there was another form of transportation available to them only 5.6% said yes. This did not surprise me too much considering that since the advent of cars in the 20th century, American cities have been designed for cars. This is often at the expense of public transportation. Sixty-one percent of respondents, however, said that it depends on the situation. The transportation authority would be hard pressed to convince those who love to drive and hate public transportation to start taking the bus, but that 61% could be swayed to take the bus more often if some of the inconveniences were addressed.

User Stories

My research indicated that 17.6% of bus riders say that the bus rarely arrives at the stop or drops them off at the next stop on time; and 23.5% of users said that the bus is on time sometimes. This frustration is echoed in the user story as each one involves arrival time in one way or another.



I addressed the problem in the user flows by including two measures to help users calculate whether or not they will make it in time to catch the bus:

  • an accurate GPS tracker which indicates how many minutes away the bus is from the stop
  • a tracker showing how many minutes away the user is from the bus stop on foot

Changes Before Usability Testing

The original about page had a sort of whimsical feel and my client preferred a more simple clean interface. The second iteration had much cleaner lines and was more simple. I included a central section for the bio for 2Birds itself, a box for each of the 2 founders, and included one where a content creator they frequently partner with could add his bio. When I showed my clients the second iteration, they remarked that they did not like that the sections for each content creator's bio was a different size and prefered that they were all the same size since they saw themselves all as equal partners.

Rather than put the team search and team mate search buttons on the page that displays information about upcoming tournaments and having those buttons direct to two separate pages, I put one button on the tournaments page which redirects the user to the team building page. On this page, the user can search for teams looking for teammates as well as for people who are looking for a team on the same page.

Usability Testing Procedure

I conducted usability tests with participants on zoom and had them share their screen so that I could watch their behavior while while they clicked around the prototype.

I prompted participants with the scenarios from my journey map and story board and asked them what they would do with my site in those scenarios.

Changes After Usability Testing

One of my usability testing participants felt that the category names in my dropdown menus were unclear.


On the content pages, I changed the name of the filter referring to menu options (ex: news) from type to category because that name option scored better in A/B testing with usability testing participants.


On the tournaments page, the status filter option became two separate buttons one for current and one for upcoming. This was because the page only gives users options to view information on current or upcoming tournaments and I felt that it was unnecessary to require users to take the extra step of selecting the status button in order to select one of those options.

One of my usability testing participants felt that the wording of the team search forum button was too casual so I switched from the question format to naming the button after the page it would direct the user to.

Accessibility Features

Ensuring that the site was accessible to any and all users was something my clients felt was important given their goal was to build a welcoming community for their followers. To ensure that the site is accessible for users with disabilities, speech and/or vision impairments, etc. I designed a menu for adjusting accessibility settings. I modeled this menu off of accessiBe's menu that I saw on the jeffreycampbell site.
