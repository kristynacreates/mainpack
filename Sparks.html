Sparks

Capstone Project
Project Details

Sparks is a dating app designed with ADHD in mind.


Why does this app matter?:

People with Attention Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder can have difficulty maintaining relationships because their behaviours are often misunderstood. Some common misunderstandings people with ADHD can experience are:

  • Fidgeting and lack of eye contact can make it seem like they are not paying attention even when spoken to directly.
  • Hyperfocusing on a certain task can cause them to lose track of time or unintentionally ignore other responsibilities.
  • Issues with time management and forgetfulness can make it seem like they do not care about their partner's feeling.
  • They may have difficulty understanding and controlling their emotions causing them to say things in anger that they later regret.

According to a short survey I conducted, 50% of respondents preferred to date people who also have ADHD; 30% said they had no preference.

ADHD symptoms show up differently in every person and of course there are situations where two ADHD people may have the same weaknesses; but there is a comfort in knowing that one's partner understands "their brain's weirdness", as one of my survey respondents put it.

Problem:

The average person spends ~9 minutes at a time and a total of ~90 minutes a day on a dating app, according to a study conducted by the dating app, Badoo, and it was not hard to figure out why.

Using dating apps can leave users feeling more frustrated (45%) than hopeful (28%), according to a study done by the Pew Research Center. The same study found that only 12% of the respondents that had used dating apps got into a serious relationship with or married someone they met on a dating app.

And as a college student gossiping in the dining hall and now after conducting interviews for this project, I have heard far more dating app horror stories than success stories.

So seeing that the average person spends so little time on dating apps and has so little luck, my challenge was designing a dating app for a group of people with behaviors that make dating difficult to begin with.

Solution:

I designed a dating app for people with ADHD that is efficient at making good matches and gives users a lot of flexibility in designing their profiles.

Audience:

This app was designed for people with ADHD of all ages, ethnicities, and gender identities.

Learnings:

In designing this app, I was able to draw from my own experience. I struggled with undiagnosed ADHD until October of 2021, so I know first hand what it feels like to restructure systems and routines in my life with the knowledge that my brain is wired differently. With that being said, because my reason for designing this app is so personal, my own bias was bound to influence my designs in a way that it couldn't if I was designing an app about something I had no previous experience with. To ensure that my design remained user centered, I made sure any design decisions could be backed up with data gathered from my surveys, the insight from the interviews I conducted, and the feedback I obtained from user testing.

In short, this project gave me a new perspective on the value of research and reminded me that our job as designers is not to create vanity projects but to create intuitive software with the end user in mind.

Discover

Beginning this project, I had two questions I was keeping in mind:

  1. What sort of design elements and features are other popular dating apps implementing to stay successful?
  2. How would a person with ADHD be helped or hindered by certain design choices and features?

To best understand what features keep users coming back, I conducted a competitive analysis on Tinder, Bumble, and Hinge. I also asked respondents in my survey to give their opinion of certain features of those dating apps (such as voice recordings and Q&A prompts in Hinge bios).

To understand how ADHD symptoms manifest and would impact a experience using my app, I collected qualitative data from surveys and during interviews.

Inspiration

The first place I drew inspiration from was a planner designed for "fast brains" that I found on Etsy.

This planner's rotating page layouts is designed to mitigate two common reasons people with ADHD abandon planners early:

  1. Feeling overwhelmed by the sheer number of tasks looming in front of them when they flip through 12 months worth of pages that all seem to blur together.
  2. Getting bored with the day to day monotony of updating the planner.

There are 8 different page designs which introduces just enough variety into the process of making your schedule to keep the brain stimulated and makes each month's schedule looks more managable when visually broken up by the varying page layouts.

Additionally, the design of each page itself encourages the user to get creative with color coding, doodling, and list making which make the planner funner to fill out and nicer to look at.

Information

50% of the respondents to my survey stated that they find themselves zoning out while swiping.

Zoning out happens frequently when reading a large block of text or looking at a bunch of pages with a repetitive format. Dating Apps like Tinder have a repetitive format which can cause users to zone out or get bored while swiping.



Implementation

Based on my observations while using this planner, I knew that I wanted for users to be able to personalize their profiles as much as possible so that they could:

  1. Construct profiles that best fit their unique personalities
  2. Avoid the monotony of dating apps in which all profiles look the same

My brainstorming sketches show my first ideas about how to break up the layout of each page.

Inspiration

The profile features I remixed in my design after completing my analysis of Hinge were the:

  1. Prompt and answer bio format
  2. Quick facts at the top of the profile (ex: religion, political affiliation)
  3. Alternating pictures and text on profiles
Information

All of the respondents to my first survey stated that they found Hinge's prompt and answer format more engaging than just reading a bio.

The greatest benefit of this format is that it allows users to find out more about their potential matches.

Implementation

I took inspiration from the prompt and answer format but rather than alternating pictures and text in rows only, I set up profiles in rows and columns. This decision was made to improve the scannability of a profile as it allows a user to:

  1. Take a quick glance at a profile and see more information at once
  2. Focus on which piece is most important to them if not all of it
  3. Make their decision efficiently.

Site Map


The site map for Sparks is pretty simple because there are not many different pages. A returning user opening the app taken to the screen displaying the profile on top of the stack of profiles to swipe through. There is no homepage because I was adhering to the mental models set by other popular dating apps like Tinder, Hinge, and Bumble.

From the unmatched profile view, users can navigate to settings or to the connections page. The connections page shows matches that the user has not messaged yet at the top of the page and recent chats take up the other 3/4ths of the page.

Changes After Usability Testing






One of my usability testing participants noticed that all of the chat bubbles in my prototype looked the same. That was of course a mistake and did not fit with the typical mental model of having different colored chat bubbles for messages sent and messages recieved.

















One of my usability testing participants noticed that my home button was not clickable. This is a mistake I have made before because I often forget to click through my entire prototype rather, I will start with each individual flow without doubling back to the homescreen.







Once I animated the home button, the back button became redundant because it would have taken users to the home page as well. Since there was no use for the back button, I added a settings button instead.

















One of my user testing participants said he felt that if he arrived at this profile editing screen right after onboarding, he wouldn't understand how a profile was supposed to look.

I added the button to view a sample profile so that a user can see an example of what "customizable" means.
